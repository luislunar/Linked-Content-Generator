If your GSA pipeline has been quiet for 12 months, your capability statement isn't the problem.

A professional services firm came to us last year: 18 months on Schedule, zero federal awards. Their NAICS codes were correct. Their past performance was solid. Their pricing catalog was the problem.

They had negotiated their GSA rates too close to their full commercial fees — no benchmarking, no Basis of Award review, no market comparison. On paper it looked compliant. In practice, they were 30–40% higher than every comparable vendor a contracting officer would pull in the same search. COs weren't even reaching page two of their quote.

We opened a contract modification and went back through their commercial records. Repriced four service lines with a proper Basis of Award analysis, documenting the volume discounts they already extended to their best clients. Prices came down. Competitive position flipped.

Within 90 days: first RFQ. Six months later: first federal award.

The GSA Schedule doesn't guarantee wins. But a badly negotiated price list guarantees losses before the comparison even starts. If your pipeline is silent after a year on Schedule, check your rates before you redesign your capability statement.
