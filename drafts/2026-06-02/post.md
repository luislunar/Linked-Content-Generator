You have a signed teaming agreement. Do you know what it actually protects you from?

Not much — unless it's drafted correctly.

Teaming agreements are letters of intent, not binding contracts. They say "if we win, here's how we'll split the work." But nothing in federal law forces a prime to honor that split once the award is made.

I've seen this pattern too many times. A small business spends months as the "key sub" on a proposal. The team wins. The prime self-performs and cuts the sub's role to 10%.

Real protection comes from a well-drafted agreement with specific workshare percentages, clear deliverables by party, and a dispute resolution clause. Not a two-paragraph email.

Before you commit to any teaming arrangement, ask one question: "Are you willing to put exact workshare percentages in writing?"

If the answer is vague, you're just on the proposal — not the team.
